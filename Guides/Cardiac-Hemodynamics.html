🫀 Section 1 — Cardiac Conduction System

Normal Conduction Pathway

Electrical impulses follow a precise route through the heart:

StructureRoleRate (intrinsic)
SA NodePrimary pacemaker — initiates impulse60–100 bpm
AV NodeDelays impulse (allows atrial filling)40–60 bpm
Bundle of HisTransmits impulse to ventricles—
Bundle BranchesR & L branches → septum & ventricles20–40 bpm
Purkinje FibersRapid depolarization of ventricles20–40 bpm
⚡
NCLEX Rule: If the SA node fails, the AV node takes over (40–60 bpm). If both fail, the ventricles generate escape rhythm (20–40 bpm) — often too slow to sustain life.

ECG Basics

Wave/IntervalRepresentsNormal
P waveAtrial depolarizationUpright, uniform, precedes QRS
PR intervalAV conduction time0.12–0.20 sec
QRS complexVentricular depolarization0.06–0.12 sec
ST segmentEarly ventricular repolarizationIsoelectric (flat)

QT intervalTotal ventricular activity0.36–0.44 sec
⚠️ ST Changes: Elevation = acute MI (STEMI). Depression = ischemia. Always correlate with troponin and patient presentation.

Cardiac Output

CO = HR × SV (Cardiac Output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume)

  • Preload — volume stretching ventricles at end-diastole (think: "volume IN")
  • Afterload — resistance the ventricles pump against (think: "pressure OUT")
  • Contractility — force of myocardial contraction
🧠 Frank-Starling Law
↑ Preload (stretch) → ↑ Stroke Volume → ↑ CO... up to a point. Overstretched fibers (severe HF) → ↓ CO. Think of a rubber band: stretch it moderately = good snap; overstretch it = weak/floppy.
💜 Section 2 — Heart Failure (HF)

Left-Sided vs. Right-Sided HF

⬅️ Left-Sided (Pulmonary)

  • Dyspnea, orthopnea, PND
  • Crackles / rales
  • Cough (frothy, pink-tinged)
  • Fatigue, ↓ exercise tolerance
  • S3 gallop (volume overload)
  • Tachycardia
  • Pulmonary edema (severe)

➡️ Right-Sided (Systemic)

  • JVD (jugular venous distention)
  • Peripheral edema (dependent)
  • Hepatomegaly, ascites
  • Weight gain
  • Anorexia, nausea
  • Increased CVP
⚡
Left = Lung. Right = Rest of the body. Left failure often leads to right failure (blood backs up into pulmonary vasculature → ↑ pulmonary pressure → right side fails).

HF Classifications

TypeEFProblemTreatment Focus
HFrEF (Systolic)<40%Weak pump — can't eject adequately↑ Contractility, ↓ preload/afterload
HFpEF (Diastolic)≥50%Stiff ventricle — can't fill adequately↓ HR (more filling time), ↓ volume

Priority Nursing Interventions

  • Daily weights — most accurate fluid status indicator (report gain >2 lbs/day or 5 lbs/week)
  • I&O monitoring, sodium restriction (≤2g/day), fluid restriction if ordered
  • Elevate HOB ≥ 30° (orthopnea, dyspnea)
  • Administer furosemide, lisinopril/enalapril, carvedilol/metoprolol succinate as ordered
  • Monitor K⁺ (diuretic risk), BNP levels, I&O, SpO₂
  • Assess lung sounds, edema, JVD Q shift
BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide): Released when ventricles are stretched. BNP >100 pg/mL = HF likely. Higher BNP = worse HF. Used to diagnose HF and monitor treatment response.

Acute MI — Time Is Muscle

Classic Presentation: Crushing substernal chest pain radiating to L arm/jaw, diaphoresis, dyspnea, nausea, sense of doom. Women & diabetics may present atypically: fatigue, indigestion, back pain, no chest pain.

Post-MI Complications

  • Dysrhythmias — #1 cause of death in first 48h; continuous telemetry
  • Cardiogenic shock — massive LV damage → ↓ CO
  • Heart failure — weakened myocardium can't pump effectively
  • Ventricular septal rupture / papillary muscle rupture — new murmur post-MI = emergency
  • Pericarditis (Dressler syndrome) — 1–8 weeks post-MI; friction rub, chest pain relieved by leaning forward

🚨 Pulseless rhythms: V-fib & Pulseless V-tach → DEFIBRILLATE. Asystole & PEA → CPR + epinephrine (no shock). Know the difference — NCLEX tests this!

A-Fib — High Yield

  • Most common sustained dysrhythmia in adults
  • Atria quiver instead of contracting → blood pools → clot formation → stroke risk
  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc score determines anticoagulation need
  • Anticoagulants: warfarin (INR 2–3), apixaban, rivaroxaban
  • Cardioversion: must anticoagulate ≥3 weeks before elective cardioversion OR rule out clot with TEE

Clinical Interpretation

ConditionCVPPAWPCOSVR
Hypovolemic Shock↓↓↓↑

⚡
CVP = right side. PAWP = left side. If BOTH are elevated with ↓ CO → cardiogenic shock (pump failure). If BOTH are low with ↓ CO → hypovolemia (tank empty). Septic shock is unique: high CO but low SVR (vasodilation).
MAP <65 mmHg = inadequate organ perfusion. Calculate: MAP = (SBP + 2×DBP) ÷ 3. Notify provider immediately if MAP drops below 65.
🔥 Section 6 — Valvular Disorders

Stenosis vs. Regurgitation

Valve ProblemPathologyHeart Sound
Mitral StenosisValve narrows → LA can't empty → LA enlarges → pulmonary congestion → A-fibRumbling diastolic murmur
Mitral RegurgitationValve leaks → blood flows back to LA during systoleBlowing systolic murmur (apex → axilla)
Aortic StenosisValve narrows → LV hypertrophy → syncope, angina, HFHarsh systolic murmur (crescendo-decrescendo)
Aortic RegurgitationValve leaks → blood flows back to LV during diastole → widened pulse pressureBlowing diastolic murmur
🧠 Murmur Timing
Stenosis/Regurgitation: Mitral Regurg & Aortic Stenosis = Systolic murmurs. Mitral Stenosis & Aortic Regurg = Diastolic murmurs. Mnemonic: MR. AS = Systolic (Mr. As → "Mr. Ass" in systole)

Aortic Stenosis — The "Triad"

Classic triad: Syncope + Angina + HF symptoms. Most dangerous valvular lesion. Sudden death risk. Avoid strenuous activity. Surgical/TAVR replacement when symptomatic.

Valve Replacement Nursing Care

  • Mechanical valves: Lifelong warfarin (INR 2.5–3.5); clicking sound is normal
  • Bioprosthetic valves: Short-term anticoagulation only; limited lifespan (10–20 yrs)
  • Endocarditis prophylaxis: Antibiotics before dental procedures for ALL prosthetic valve patients

Beta-Blockers (-olol)

DrugKey UseCritical Nursing Points
MetoprololHF, HTN, post-MIHold if HR <60 or SBP <90; do NOT stop abruptly (rebound tachycardia)
CarvedilolHF (α & β blocker)Take with food; monitor for orthostatic hypotension

⚠️ Beta-blocker contraindications: Uncompensated HF, severe bradycardia, 2°/3° heart block, severe asthma/COPD. Mask hypoglycemia in diabetics (no tachycardia warning).

ACE Inhibitors (-pril) & ARBs (-sartan)

ClassExamplesKey Points
ACE-ILisinopril, enalaprilDry cough (bradykinin). Monitor K⁺ (hyperkalemia). Avoid in pregnancy.

⚡
First-dose hypotension: ACE-I/ARBs can cause a dramatic BP drop with the first dose. Give at bedtime initially. Hold if SBP <90. Monitor renal function (BUN/Cr) and K⁺.

Digoxin

  • ↑ Contractility (positive inotrope), ↓ HR (negative chronotrope)
  • Therapeutic range: 0.5–2.0 ng/mL (narrow)
  • Hold if HR <60 (adults) or <70 (children)
  • Check K⁺ before giving — hypokalemia = ↑ toxicity risk
  • Toxicity signs: N/V, visual disturbances (yellow/green halos), bradycardia, heart block
  • Antidote: Digibind (digoxin immune Fab)

Nitroglycerin

  • SL: 1 tab q5min × 3 doses max. If no relief after 1st dose → call 911
  • Store in dark glass container; replace q6 months; no cotton in bottle
  • Headache = expected (vasodilation)
  • Contraindicated with sildenafil/PDE5 inhibitors (severe hypotension)
  • IV NTG: use glass bottles and special non-PVC tubing
