💧 Section 1 — Fluid Balance & Body Compartments

Total Body Water Distribution

Water makes up approximately 60% of adult body weight. It is distributed across two main compartments:

CompartmentAbbreviation% of Body WaterLocation
IntracellularICF~2/3 (≈ 40% body wt)Inside cells
ExtracellularECF~1/3 (≈ 20% body wt)Outside cells
  — IntravascularIVF~5% body wtPlasma (blood vessels)
  — InterstitialISF~15% body wtBetween cells & vessels
  — TranscellularTCF~1-2%CSF, pleural, peritoneal

Osmolality & Fluid Movement

Serum osmolality measures the concentration of dissolved particles in blood. Normal range:

⬇ <275 ✅ 275 – 295 mOsm/kg ⬆ >295
  • Water follows solute — fluid moves from LOW osmolality (dilute) to HIGH osmolality (concentrated)
  • Osmosis = passive movement of water across semipermeable membrane
  • ADH (antidiuretic hormone) = released when osmolality rises → kidneys retain water → dilutes serum
  • Aldosterone = retains Na⁺ (and water follows) → increases intravascular volume

Fluid Volume Deficit (FVD) — Dehydration / Hypovolemia

📋 Signs & Symptoms

  • ↓ BP, ↑ HR (tachycardia)
  • Weak, thready pulse
  • Poor skin turgor (tenting)
  • Dry mucous membranes
  • Concentrated urine (↑ specific gravity >1.030)
  • ↓ Urine output (<30 mL/hr)
  • Weight loss (acute)
  • ↑ BUN:creatinine ratio (>20:1)
  • Flat neck veins when supine

🏥 Nursing Priorities

  • Replace fluids — isotonic IV (NS or LR)
  • Monitor I&O strictly
  • Daily weights (same time, same scale)
  • Assess orthostatic vital signs
  • Safety: fall precautions (dizziness)
  • Oral rehydration if tolerating PO
  • Monitor serum electrolytes & BUN

Fluid Volume Excess (FVE) — Hypervolemia / Overload

📋 Signs & Symptoms

  • ↑ BP, bounding pulse
  • Weight gain (rapid)
  • Edema (peripheral, periorbital)
  • Distended neck veins (JVD)
  • Crackles / rales in lungs
  • Dyspnea, orthopnea
  • Dilute urine (↓ specific gravity)
  • ↓ Hematocrit (hemodilution)

🏥 Nursing Priorities

  • Restrict fluids & sodium
  • Administer furosemide (loop diuretic) as ordered
  • Elevate HOB ≥ 30°
  • Monitor I&O, daily weights
  • Auscultate lungs Q4H
  • Assess for pulmonary edema
  • Monitor potassium (diuretic risk)
⚡
NCLEX Rule: 1 liter of fluid ≈ 1 kg (2.2 lbs). A gain of 2 lbs overnight = ~1 L fluid retention. Daily weights are the most accurate measure of fluid status.

Third-Spacing

Fluid shifts from intravascular space into interstitial tissues or body cavities (peritoneum, pleural space) where it cannot be used. Common in burns, liver failure, pancreatitis, and post-surgical patients.

  • Paradox: Patient is edematous (total body fluid ↑) but intravascularly depleted (↓ BP, ↑ HR)
  • Treatment: IV fluid replacement + treat underlying cause
  • Monitor for reabsorption phase (24-72 hrs) — fluid returns to vessels → risk for FVE
🧂 Section 2 — Sodium (Na⁺)

Normal Range

⬇ <136 ✅ 136 – 145 mEq/L ⬆ >145

Sodium is the most abundant extracellular cation. It regulates fluid balance, nerve impulses, and muscle contraction. Where Na⁺ goes, water follows.

Hyponatremia (<136 mEq/L)

⚠️ Critical value: <120 mEq/L — risk of cerebral edema, seizures, coma, death

⬇️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Confusion, headache, lethargy
  • Muscle weakness & cramps
  • Nausea & vomiting
  • Seizures (severe)
  • ↓ Deep tendon reflexes
  • Abdominal cramping

🏥 Treatment

  • Fluid restriction (dilutional)
  • Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) for severe (<120)
  • Correct slowly — max 8-12 mEq/L per 24 hrs
  • Monitor for osmotic demyelination syndrome
  • Treat underlying cause (SIADH, water intoxication)
  • Seizure precautions
⚡
NCLEX Rule: Correcting Na⁺ too rapidly causes osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS) — irreversible brain damage. Rate must be ≤12 mEq/L in 24 hours.

Hypernatremia (>145 mEq/L)

⚠️ Critical value: >160 mEq/L — risk of cellular dehydration, seizures

⬆️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Extreme thirst
  • Dry, flushed skin
  • Fever (low-grade)
  • Restlessness → agitation → seizures
  • Muscle twitching & rigidity
  • ↓ Urine output (concentrated)

🏥 Treatment

  • Hypotonic IV solutions (0.45% NaCl) or D5W
  • Increase free water intake (PO)
  • Correct slowly — ↓ Na⁺ no faster than 0.5 mEq/L/hr
  • Monitor for cerebral edema
  • Low-sodium diet
  • Assess neurologic status Q1-2H

SIADH vs. Diabetes Insipidus

💜 Section 3 — Potassium (K⁺)

Normal Range

⬇ <3.5 ✅ 3.5 – 5.0 mEq/L ⬆ >5.0

Potassium is the major intracellular cation. Even small changes are life-threatening because K⁺ directly affects cardiac conduction. Only 2% of total body K⁺ is in the blood.

⚠️ CRITICAL: Always check K⁺ before giving digoxin. Hypokalemia + digoxin = digoxin toxicity. Hold digoxin if K⁺ <3.5.

Hypokalemia (<3.5 mEq/L)

⬇️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Muscle weakness → flaccidity
  • Fatigue & lethargy
  • ↓ Bowel sounds → paralytic ileus
  • Shallow respirations
  • Weak, irregular pulse
  • Leg cramps
  • ECG: flattened T waves, U waves, ST depression

🏥 Treatment

  • Oral K⁺ replacement (preferred if not critical)
  • IV potassium chloride (KCl) — NEVER push IV
  • Max rate: 10-20 mEq/hr via pump ONLY
  • Must dilute; infuse into large vein
  • Monitor telemetry during IV infusion
  • K⁺-rich foods: bananas, oranges, potatoes, spinach, avocados
  • Check Mg²⁺ — hypomagnesemia prevents K⁺ correction
🚨 NEVER push IV potassium! It can cause fatal cardiac arrest. Must always be diluted and infused on a pump with cardiac monitoring.

Hyperkalemia (>5.0 mEq/L)

⚠️ Critical: >6.5 mEq/L — risk of fatal cardiac dysrhythmias (V-fib, asystole)

⬆️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Muscle twitching → weakness → paralysis
  • Paresthesias (tingling)
  • Nausea, diarrhea, cramping
  • Bradycardia → heart block
  • ECG: tall, peaked T waves, wide QRS, absent P waves
  • Cardiac arrest

🏥 Treatment — Act FAST

  • 1. Protect the heart: calcium gluconate IV (stabilizes myocardium)
  • 2. Shift K⁺ into cells: Regular insulin + D50W, sodium bicarbonate, nebulized albuterol
  • 3. Remove K⁺ from body: sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate), patiromer, loop diuretics
  • 4. Dialysis if refractory
  • Avoid K⁺-rich foods
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring
🧠 Memory: Potassium & the Heart
Hypo K⁺ → Flat T waves & U waves ("I'm Flat and feel Unwell")
Hyper K⁺ → Tall, Peaked T waves ("The Tent is getting Tall — Murder on the heart")

Potassium & pH Relationship

⚡
K⁺ and H⁺ are BFFs — they trade places across cell membranes. In acidosis, H⁺ moves INTO cells, K⁺ moves OUT → hyperkalemia. In alkalosis, H⁺ moves OUT, K⁺ moves IN → hypokalemia. Always check pH when interpreting K⁺ levels!
🦴 Section 4 — Calcium (Ca²⁺)

Normal Range (Total Calcium)

⬇ <9.0 ✅ 9.0 – 10.5 mg/dL ⬆ >10.5

Calcium is critical for bone structure, muscle contraction, nerve transmission, and blood clotting. It has an inverse relationship with phosphorus. Regulated by PTH (parathyroid hormone) and calcitonin.

HormoneSourceAction on Ca²⁺Mnemonic
PTHParathyroid↑ Ca²⁺ (pulls from bones)PTH = "Push The calcium Higher"
CalcitoninThyroid C cells↓ Ca²⁺ (stores in bones)CalciTONin TONes it down

Hypocalcemia (<9.0 mg/dL)

⬇️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Tetany — muscle spasms, tingling
  • Trousseau sign (+) → hand spasm with BP cuff
  • Chvostek sign (+) → facial twitch (tap CN VII)
  • Hyperactive DTRs
  • Numbness/tingling around lips & fingers
  • Laryngospasm (severe)
  • Seizures (severe)
  • ECG: prolonged QT interval

🏥 Treatment

  • IV calcium gluconate (preferred) or calcium chloride
  • Oral calcium + vitamin D supplements
  • Seizure precautions
  • Airway equipment at bedside (laryngospasm risk)
  • Administer SLOWLY IV (cardiac monitoring)
  • Ca²⁺-rich foods: dairy, leafy greens, sardines
🧠 Trousseau & Chvostek
Trousseau = Trousers → hand (carpal spasm with BP cuff)
Chvostek = Cheek → facial twitch when tapped

Hypercalcemia (>10.5 mg/dL)

⚠️ Critical: >13 mg/dL — cardiac arrest risk

⬆️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Muscle weakness & fatigue
  • ↓ Deep tendon reflexes
  • Constipation, anorexia, N/V
  • Polyuria → kidney stones
  • Confusion → lethargy → coma
  • Pathologic fractures
  • ECG: shortened QT interval

🏥 Treatment

  • NS hydration (dilute & promote excretion)
  • calcitonin (rapid onset, short-lived)
  • zoledronic acid / bisphosphonates (long-term)
  • Loop diuretics (furosemide) — NOT thiazides
  • Dialysis if severe/refractory
  • Avoid thiazide diuretics (retain Ca²⁺)
  • Weight-bearing exercise, low-calcium diet
⚡
NCLEX Rule: Thiazide diuretics increase calcium reabsorption (worsen hypercalcemia). Loop diuretics (furosemide) promote calcium excretion. Mnemonic: "Loops Lose calcium."
🧠 Hypercalcemia: "Moans, Bones, Stones, Groans"
Moans — confusion, lethargy
Bones — bone pain, pathologic fractures
Stones — renal calculi (kidney stones)
Groans — constipation, abdominal pain
🔥 Section 5 — Magnesium (Mg²⁺)

Normal Range

⬇ <1.3 ✅ 1.3 – 2.1 mg/dL ⬆ >2.1

Magnesium is the "forgotten electrolyte" — essential for neuromuscular function, enzyme activation, and maintaining K⁺ and Ca²⁺ balance. Mg²⁺ works closely with potassium: you cannot fix hypokalemia if Mg²⁺ is also low.

Hypomagnesemia (<1.3 mg/dL)

⬇️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Hyperactive DTRs
  • Tremors, muscle cramps
  • Positive Trousseau & Chvostek (same as ↓Ca²⁺)
  • Insomnia, agitation
  • Dysphagia
  • Cardiac dysrhythmias
  • Torsades de pointes
  • Seizures (severe)

🏥 Treatment

  • IV magnesium sulfate (MgSO₄) — infuse slowly
  • Oral magnesium oxide
  • Monitor DTRs during infusion
  • Cardiac monitoring
  • Replace K⁺ and Ca²⁺ if also low
  • Mg²⁺-rich foods: nuts, greens, whole grains, chocolate
  • Common causes: alcoholism, diuretics, malnutrition
⚡
NCLEX Rule: Hypomagnesemia looks like hypocalcemia (positive Trousseau & Chvostek). Always check Mg²⁺ when K⁺ is persistently low and won't correct — Mg²⁺ is the "gatekeeper" of K⁺.

Hypermagnesemia (>2.1 mg/dL)

⚠️ Critical: >7 mg/dL — cardiac arrest, respiratory paralysis

⬆️ Signs & Symptoms

  • ↓ or absent DTRs (earliest sign)
  • Muscle weakness → flaccidity
  • Lethargy, drowsiness
  • Hypotension, bradycardia
  • Flushing & warmth
  • N/V
  • Respiratory depression (late)
  • Cardiac arrest (very late)

🏥 Treatment

  • STOP all Mg²⁺ sources
  • IV calcium gluconate (antagonist)
  • IV fluids + loop diuretics
  • Mechanical ventilation PRN
  • Dialysis if renal failure
  • Monitor DTRs frequently
  • Keep calcium gluconate at bedside during MgSO₄ infusions
OB Connection: Magnesium sulfate is used to treat preeclampsia/eclampsia. Toxic levels cause ↓ DTRs → respiratory arrest. Antidote: calcium gluconate. Check DTRs Q1H, urine output ≥ 30 mL/hr, respirations ≥ 12/min.
🩷 Section 6 — Phosphorus (PO₄³⁻)

Normal Range

⬇ <3.0 ✅ 3.0 – 4.5 mg/dL ⬆ >4.5

Phosphorus is critical for ATP production, bone mineralization, and acid-base buffering. It has an inverse (seesaw) relationship with calcium: when one goes up, the other goes down.

⚡
Calcium-Phosphorus Seesaw: ↑ Ca²⁺ = ↓ PO₄ and ↑ PO₄ = ↓ Ca²⁺. This is HIGH-YIELD for NCLEX — especially in renal failure (↑ PO₄ → ↓ Ca²⁺ → bone loss).

Hypophosphatemia (<3.0 mg/dL)

⬇️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Muscle weakness
  • Confusion, irritability
  • Numbness & tingling
  • Bone pain, pathologic fractures
  • Chest muscle weakness → respiratory failure
  • ↓ Platelet function (bleeding risk)
  • Rhabdomyolysis (severe)

🏥 Treatment

  • Oral phosphorus supplements
  • IV sodium phosphate or potassium phosphate (severe)
  • PO₄-rich foods: dairy, meat, nuts, beans, whole grains
  • Avoid aluminum-based antacids (bind phosphorus)
  • Monitor Ca²⁺ (inverse relationship)
  • Common cause: refeeding syndrome, DKA treatment
Refeeding Syndrome: When malnourished patients resume nutrition, insulin surge drives PO₄ (and K⁺, Mg²⁺) into cells → severe hypophosphatemia → cardiac failure, respiratory failure, death. Feed slowly! Monitor electrolytes Q4-6H.

Hyperphosphatemia (>4.5 mg/dL)

⬆️ Signs & Symptoms

  • Signs of hypocalcemia (seesaw effect)
  • Tetany, muscle spasms
  • Soft tissue calcification
  • Pruritus (itching)
  • Often asymptomatic until severe

🏥 Treatment

  • Phosphate binders: sevelamer, calcium acetate
  • Take binders WITH meals
  • Low-phosphorus diet
  • Dialysis (renal patients)
  • Treat underlying cause (usually CKD)
  • Monitor Ca²⁺ levels concurrently
💉 Section 7 — IV Fluids & Solutions

Tonicity Overview

IV solutions are categorized by how their osmolarity compares to blood plasma (≈ 275-295 mOsm/L):

Isotonic

Same osmolarity as blood

Stays in intravascular space

NS (0.9% NaCl), LR, D5W*

Hypotonic

Lower osmolarity than blood

Shifts INTO cells

0.45% NaCl (½ NS), 0.225% NaCl (¼ NS)

Hypertonic

Higher osmolarity than blood

Pulls OUT of cells

3% NaCl, 5% NaCl, D10W, D5NS, D5LR
D5W Note: D5W is initially isotonic in the bag but becomes hypotonic once infused because the body metabolizes the dextrose quickly, leaving only free water.

Solution Details

SolutionTypeUseCautions

Lactated Ringer's (LR)IsotonicBurns, GI losses, surgical replacementAvoid in liver failure (can't metabolize lactate); NEVER with blood products
D5WIsotonic → HypotonicDehydration, med dilution, TKOAvoid in ↑ ICP (worsens cerebral edema)
0.45% NaCl (½ NS)HypotonicCellular dehydration, hypernatremia, DKA maintenanceNEVER give with ↑ ICP
3% NaClHypertonicSevere hyponatremia (<120), ↑ ICP (pulls fluid from brain)Must infuse via pump; risk of FVE, pulmonary edema

⚡
NCLEX Rule — Increased ICP: Use hypertonic solutions (3% NaCl) or mannitol to pull fluid OUT of brain cells. NEVER give hypotonic or D5W solutions — they cause cells to swell → herniation.

Hypo vs. Hyper — Universal Patterns

🧠 General Patterns for NCLEX
Hypo levels → hyperexcitable nerves & muscles (twitching, spasms, seizures, ↑ DTRs)
Hyper levels → hypoexcitable (weakness, flaccidity, ↓ DTRs, lethargy)

Exception: HypoK⁺ causes weakness (not excitability) because K⁺ affects cardiac & skeletal muscle directly
