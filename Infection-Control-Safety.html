🦠 Infection Control & Safety

Standard Precautions • Transmission-Based Isolation • Sterile Technique • TB • HAIs • Neutropenic Care

1 · Standard & Transmission-Based Precautions
Standard Precautions — Applied to ALL Patients
  • Treat ALL blood, body fluids, secretions, excretions (except sweat), non-intact skin, and mucous membranes as potentially infectious
  • Hand hygiene before & after EVERY patient contact
  • Gloves for contact with blood/body fluids; change between tasks on same patient
  • Gown, mask, eye protection when splash/spray risk exists
  • Safe injection practices — one needle, one syringe, one patient
  • Respiratory hygiene / cough etiquette for all persons with respiratory symptoms
NCLEX KEY: Standard Precautions are used for EVERY patient regardless of diagnosis. They are the foundation — transmission-based precautions are ADDED on top.

PPE Donning & Doffing Order

Donning (putting ON) — "Going to war":

1. Gown → 2. Mask/N95 → 3. Goggles/Shield → 4. Gloves

Doffing (taking OFF) — "Most contaminated first":

1. Gloves → 2. Goggles/Shield → 3. Gown → 4. Mask/N95
Remember: Gloves go on LAST (donning) and come off FIRST (doffing). The mask is the LAST item removed. Hand hygiene is performed after removing EACH piece of PPE and again after all PPE is removed.
2 · Sterile Technique & Surgical Asepsis
Sterile Field Principles — The Non-Negotiables
Rule Rationale / NCLEX Application
1-inch border rule The outer 1 inch of a sterile drape is considered CONTAMINATED. Never place sterile items within this border zone.
Never turn your back Turning away from the sterile field = you can no longer verify it's uncontaminated. If you look away, the field is considered contaminated.
Wet = contaminated Moisture allows microorganisms to wick through fabric (strike-through contamination). A wet sterile field must be replaced.
Below waist = contaminated Anything below the waist or below the level of the sterile field is considered non-sterile. Keep hands above waist at all times.
Sterile-to-sterile only Only sterile items can touch other sterile items. A non-sterile item touching a sterile surface contaminates the entire field.
Open packages away from you Open the far flap first, then sides, then the flap nearest you. This prevents reaching over the sterile area.
Pour without splashing When pouring solutions, hold the bottle 4-6 inches above the receptacle. Pour away from the label. Do NOT recap or reuse opened bottles.
Sterile vs. Clean Technique
Sterile (Surgical Asepsis)
  • Insertion of urinary catheters
  • Surgical procedures
  • Central line dressing changes
  • Tracheostomy suctioning
  • Wound irrigation (deep wounds)
  • Lumbar puncture assist
Clean (Medical Asepsis)
  • Nasopharyngeal suctioning
  • Vital signs / physical assessment
  • Enema administration
  • Ostomy care
  • Bathing / hygiene care
  • Oral medication administration
NCLEX TIP: When in doubt, choose the MORE sterile option. Any break in sterile technique requires you to STOP and start over with new sterile supplies.
3 · Healthcare-Associated Infections (HAIs)
Central Line–Associated Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI) Prevention
🩸 CLABSI Prevention Bundle
  • Hand hygiene before insertion and during dressing changes
  • Full barrier precautions during insertion (cap, mask, sterile gown, sterile gloves, full-body drape)
  • Chlorhexidine skin antisepsis (chlorhexidine 2% scrub — NOT povidone-iodine for central lines)
  • Optimal catheter site selection — subclavian preferred (lowest infection rate); avoid femoral (highest infection rate)
  • Daily review of line necessity — remove as soon as no longer needed
  • Transparent dressing changed every 7 days (or gauze every 2 days)
Catheter-Associated Urinary Tract Infection (CAUTI) Prevention
🚿 CAUTI Prevention Bundle
  • Insert ONLY when clinically indicated (not for convenience or monitoring I&O)
  • Strict sterile technique during insertion
  • Maintain a closed drainage system — never disconnect the catheter from tubing
  • Keep bag BELOW the level of the bladder at all times — never raise above
  • Perform perineal hygiene with soap and water (NOT betadine)
  • Daily assessment for continued need — remove as early as possible
  • Secure the catheter to prevent tension/traction on the urethra
Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP) Prevention
🫁 VAP Prevention Bundle
  • Elevate HOB 30-45 degrees (reduces aspiration)
  • Oral care with chlorhexidine every 12 hours
  • Daily sedation vacation (spontaneous awakening trial)
  • Daily spontaneous breathing trial (assess readiness to extubate)
  • DVT and PUD prophylaxis
  • Endotracheal tube cuff pressure maintained at 20-30 cm H₂O
C. difficile (C. diff) — Special Considerations
Key Point Detail
Hand Hygiene Soap and water ONLY — alcohol-based hand sanitizer does NOT kill C. diff spores. This is an NCLEX favorite!
Precautions CONTACT precautions: gloves + gown. Private room. Dedicated equipment (stethoscope, BP cuff).
Cleaning Bleach-based (sodium hypochlorite) disinfectant for environmental surfaces. Quaternary ammonium compounds are INEFFECTIVE against spores.
Cause Disruption of normal flora from broad-spectrum antibiotics (clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins)
Treatment Stop offending antibiotic. First-line: oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin. Metronidazole (Flagyl) for mild cases. IV vancomycin is NOT effective for C. diff (doesn't reach the gut lumen).
Key Symptom Profuse, watery, foul-smelling diarrhea (often described as "horse barn" odor)
HIGH-YIELD: NCLEX frequently tests: "After caring for a patient with C. diff, the nurse should perform hand hygiene using _____." Answer: Soap and water — NEVER alcohol-based sanitizer for C. diff.
Surgical Site Infection (SSI) Prevention
  • Administer prophylactic antibiotics within 1 hour before surgical incision (or 2 hours for vancomycin/fluoroquinolones)
  • Clip hair (do NOT shave) — shaving creates micro-abrasions that harbor bacteria
  • Maintain normothermia during surgery
  • Maintain blood glucose control (especially in diabetic patients)
  • Sterile technique for dressing changes for first 24-48 hours
  • Teach patient signs of infection: redness, warmth, swelling, purulent drainage, fever
4 · Tuberculosis Screening & Management
TB Testing Methods
Test Key Details
TST (Mantoux / PPD) Intradermal injection of 0.1 mL purified protein derivative on the inner forearm. Read in 48-72 hours. Measure only induration (not redness). A trained reader palpates and measures the raised, hardened area in mm.
TST Interpretation ≥5 mm = positive for: HIV+, immunosuppressed, recent TB contacts, abnormal CXR
≥10 mm = positive for: healthcare workers, high-risk groups (homeless, IVDU, foreign-born from endemic areas)
≥15 mm = positive for: anyone (low risk, no known risk factors)
IGRA (Blood test) QuantiFERON-TB Gold or T-SPOT. Blood draw, one visit. Preferred for BCG-vaccinated individuals (TST may cause false-positive in BCG recipients).
NCLEX TIP: A positive TST or IGRA means exposure/infection, NOT active disease. A chest X-ray and sputum culture are needed to differentiate latent vs active TB. Three consecutive negative sputum AFB smears = may discontinue airborne precautions.
Latent TB vs. Active TB
Latent TB (LTBI)
  • Positive TST/IGRA
  • Normal chest X-ray
  • No symptoms
  • NOT contagious
  • Negative sputum cultures
  • Treat: isoniazid (INH) × 9 months OR rifampin × 4 months
Active TB
  • Positive TST/IGRA
  • Abnormal chest X-ray (infiltrates, cavitation)
  • Symptoms: persistent cough >3 weeks, hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss, fever
  • CONTAGIOUS — airborne precautions required
  • Positive sputum AFB cultures
  • Treat: RIPE therapy (see below)
RIPE Therapy — Active TB Treatment
💊 RIPE — First-Line TB Medications
  • Rifampin — turns body fluids orange/red (urine, tears, sweat). Decreases effectiveness of oral contraceptives. Potent CYP450 inducer.
  • Isoniazid (INH) — hepatotoxic (monitor LFTs). Give with pyridoxine (Vitamin B6) to prevent peripheral neuropathy.
  • Pyrazinamide — hepatotoxic. Monitor uric acid levels (can cause hyperuricemia/gout).
  • Ethambutol — causes optic neuritis (visual changes: decreased acuity, color blindness). Baseline vision exam required. "E = Eyes"
CRITICAL: TB treatment requires DIRECTLY OBSERVED THERAPY (DOT) — the nurse watches the patient swallow every dose. Non-compliance leads to multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB), a public health emergency.
Airborne Precautions for TB
  • N95 respirator required for all staff entering the room (must be fit-tested annually)
  • Negative-pressure airborne infection isolation room (AIIR) — minimum 6-12 air exchanges per hour
  • Door remains CLOSED at all times
  • Patient wears a surgical mask (NOT N95) when transported outside the room
  • Discontinue airborne precautions after 3 consecutive negative sputum AFB smears collected 8-24 hours apart
5 · Bloodborne Pathogen Exposure
Needlestick Protocol — Immediate Actions
Step Action
1 Wash the site immediately with soap and water for 15 minutes. For mucous membrane exposure, flush with water or saline. Do NOT squeeze or "milk" the wound.
2 Report the incident to the supervisor/charge nurse immediately
3 Complete an incident/occurrence report
4 Test the source patient (with consent): HIV, Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C
5 Obtain baseline labs on the exposed nurse: HIV, HBV, HCV
6 Initiate post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) if indicated
HIV Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)
  • Must begin within 72 hours of exposure (ideally within 1-2 hours)
  • 28-day course of antiretroviral therapy (typically 3-drug regimen)
  • Follow-up HIV testing: 6 weeks, 12 weeks, 6 months post-exposure
  • If source patient is HIV-negative, PEP is generally NOT recommended
Hepatitis B Post-Exposure Management
Exposed Nurse Status Action
Vaccinated & immune
(anti-HBs ≥10 mIU/mL)		 No treatment needed — the nurse is protected
Vaccinated but non-immune
(anti-HBs <10 mIU/mL)		 HBIG (Hepatitis B Immune Globulin) + restart HBV vaccine series
Unvaccinated HBIG within 24 hours + begin HBV vaccine series immediately
KEY POINT: There is NO post-exposure prophylaxis for Hepatitis C — only monitoring and early treatment if seroconversion occurs. HCV follow-up: test at 4-6 weeks post-exposure.
Sharps Safety
  • NEVER recap needles (the #1 cause of needlestick injuries)
  • Activate safety devices immediately after use
  • Dispose in puncture-resistant sharps containers at the point of use
  • Sharps containers are replaced when ¾ full (NEVER overfilled)
  • Never reach into a sharps container to retrieve items
6 · Neutropenic & Immunocompromised Precautions
Absolute Neutrophil Count (ANC) Calculation
🧮 ANC Formula
  • ANC = WBC × (% Neutrophils + % Bands) / 100
  • Normal ANC: >1,500 cells/mm³
  • Mild neutropenia: 1,000–1,500
  • Moderate neutropenia: 500–1,000
  • Severe neutropenia: <500 — HIGH risk for life-threatening infection
Example: WBC = 3,000, Neutrophils = 40%, Bands = 5%
ANC = 3,000 × (40 + 5) / 100 = 3,000 × 0.45 = 1,350 → Mild neutropenia
Neutropenic Precautions
Category Nursing Interventions
Room Private room with positive pressure (air flows OUT of the room to protect the patient). HEPA filtration. Limit visitors. No flowers or standing water (harbor Aspergillus).
Monitoring Monitor temperature every 4 hours. A temp ≥100.4°F (38°C) in a neutropenic patient is a MEDICAL EMERGENCY — obtain blood cultures and start broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour (febrile neutropenia protocol).
Diet Neutropenic (low-microbial) diet: No raw fruits/vegetables (unless thick-skinned and peeled), no raw/undercooked meat, no unpasteurized dairy, no deli meats, no sushi. All food must be thoroughly cooked.
Hygiene Strict hand hygiene by ALL persons entering the room. Soft toothbrush to prevent gum bleeding/breaks. No rectal temperatures, suppositories, or enemas. No IM injections if avoidable.
Visitors Screen visitors for illness. No visitors with respiratory infections, recent vaccinations with live viruses, or active herpes simplex.
NCLEX CRITICAL: TB = Negative pressure (protect others FROM the patient). Neutropenia = Positive pressure (protect the patient FROM others). This is a top-tested comparison!
Key Medications for Neutropenia
  • Filgrastim (Neupogen) / Pegfilgrastim (Neulasta) — Colony-stimulating factors (G-CSF) that stimulate bone marrow to produce neutrophils. Monitor for bone pain (most common side effect).
  • Prophylactic antibiotics/antifungals may be ordered for severe neutropenia (ANC <500)
7 · High-Alert Infection Control Medications
Medication Key Points Nursing Considerations
Vancomycin (IV) Glycopeptide antibiotic for MRSA, serious gram-positive infections Monitor trough levels (15-20 mcg/mL) for serious infections. Red Man Syndrome from rapid infusion → slow the rate (infuse over ≥60 min). Monitor renal function & hearing (ototoxic + nephrotoxic).
Gentamicin / Tobramycin (aminoglycosides) Gram-negative coverage, often combined with other antibiotics Monitor peak & trough levels. Nephrotoxic (monitor BUN/creatinine) and Ototoxic (monitor hearing, tinnitus, balance). Never mix with penicillins in same IV line.
Isoniazid (INH) First-line TB treatment & prophylaxis Hepatotoxic — monitor LFTs monthly. ALWAYS give with Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) to prevent peripheral neuropathy. Avoid alcohol, tyramine-rich foods (MAO inhibitor properties).
Rifampin TB treatment, also used for latent TB and meningococcal prophylaxis Turns body fluids orange/red (counsel patient). Potent CYP450 inducer — reduces effectiveness of warfarin, oral contraceptives, HIV medications. Monitor LFTs.
Acyclovir / Valacyclovir Antiviral for herpes simplex, varicella-zoster Ensure adequate hydration (2-3 L/day) to prevent crystalluria and renal damage. Most effective when started within 72 hours of symptom onset.
Metronidazole (Flagyl) Anaerobic bacteria, C. diff (mild cases), protozoal infections No alcohol — severe disulfiram-like reaction (nausea, vomiting, flushing, headache). Metallic taste is common. Dark/red-brown urine is expected.
Amphotericin B Systemic antifungal for serious fungal infections Known as "Ampho-terrible" for severe side effects: infusion reactions (fever, chills, rigors), nephrotoxicity (monitor BUN/creatinine, potassium, magnesium), hypokalemia. Premedicate with acetaminophen, diphenhydramine, and meperidine for rigors.
Tamiflu (Oseltamivir) Antiviral for influenza A and B Must start within 48 hours of symptom onset for effectiveness. Can be used for treatment AND prophylaxis. GI side effects common.
