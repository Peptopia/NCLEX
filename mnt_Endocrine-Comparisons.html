1 🦋 Thyroid Disorders
🧊 Hypothyroidism VS 🔥 Hyperthyroidism
Memory Aid — Think METABOLISM:
Hypothyroidism = Everything slows DOWN ↓ (cold, constipated, slow HR, weight gain, sluggish)
Hyperthyroidism = Everything speeds UP ↑ (hot, diarrhea, fast HR, weight loss, wired)
Feature 🧊 Hypothyroidism 🔥 Hyperthyroidism
Pathophysiology Insufficient thyroid hormone (T3/T4) production. Metabolism slows globally. Most common cause: Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune destruction of thyroid gland). Excess thyroid hormone (T3/T4) production. Metabolism accelerates uncontrollably. Most common cause: Grave's disease (autoimmune stimulation of thyroid via TSI antibodies).
Vitals HR (bradycardia), BP, Temp, RR HR (tachycardia), BP (systolic HTN), Temp, RR
Weight Weight gain (despite poor appetite) — metabolism slowed Weight loss (despite increased appetite) — metabolism revved
Temperature Cold intolerance — always cold, wears extra layers Heat intolerance — always hot, diaphoretic, sweating
Cardiac Bradycardia, ↓ cardiac output, possible pericardial effusion, ↑ cholesterol Tachycardia, palpitations, atrial fibrillation, widened pulse pressure, HF risk
GI Constipation, decreased appetite, possible ileus Diarrhea, increased appetite, hyperactive bowel sounds, weight loss
Neuro/Psych Fatigue, lethargy, depression, slowed cognition, memory problems, slow speech Anxiety, irritability, insomnia, restlessness, fine tremors, emotional lability, short attention span
Skin/Hair Dry/coarse skin, brittle nails, hair loss, non-pitting edema (myxedema), puffy face Warm/moist/smooth skin, fine hair, pretibial myxedema (Grave's), diaphoresis
Eyes Periorbital edema (puffy eyelids) Exophthalmos (bulging eyes — Grave's specific). Lid lag, lid retraction. May be permanent even after treatment.
Neck Possible goiter (if iodine deficiency or Hashimoto's) Diffuse goiter (Grave's), thyroid bruit (audible with stethoscope due to ↑ blood flow)
Reproductive Menorrhagia (heavy periods), decreased libido, infertility Amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea (light/absent periods), decreased libido
Musculoskeletal Muscle cramps, weakness, joint stiffness, carpal tunnel Muscle wasting, weakness, osteoporosis risk, hyperreflexia
🧪 TSH ↑ ELEVATED (pituitary screaming for more T4) ↓ LOW (pituitary suppressed by excess T4)
🧪 T3 / T4 ↓ LOW (the primary deficiency) ↑ ELEVATED (the primary excess)
🧪 Cholesterol ↑ Elevated (slowed lipid metabolism) ↓ Low-normal
🧪 Glucose Hypoglycemia risk Hyperglycemia risk
Electrolytes ↓ Na+ (dilutional hyponatremia), ↑ CO2 ↑ Ca++ (bone resorption), ↓ Mg++
💊 Treatment Levothyroxine (Synthroid) — thyroid hormone replacement. Lifelong therapy.
• Take on empty stomach, 30-60 min before breakfast
• Same time daily, same brand
• Monitor TSH every 6-8 weeks initially		 PTU (Propylthiouracil) or Methimazole (Tapazole) — antithyroid drugs
Radioactive iodine (I-131) — ablation therapy
Beta-blockers (Propranolol) — symptomatic tachycardia
Thyroidectomy — surgical removal
NCLEX Key: TSH and T4 are inverse in primary thyroid disease. If TSH is HIGH, thyroid hormones are LOW (hypothyroid). If TSH is LOW, thyroid hormones are HIGH (hyperthyroid). Think: TSH is the thermostat — it cranks up when the furnace (thyroid) isn't working.

🔥 Grave's Disease — Deep Dive

HYPERTHYROID AUTOIMMUNE

Pathophysiology: Autoimmune disorder where thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSI) mimic TSH, causing the thyroid to overproduce T3/T4 uncontrollably. Most common cause of hyperthyroidism. Affects women 5-10× more than men.

Unique Grave's Features (The "Grave's Triad"):

  • Diffuse goiter — symmetrically enlarged, non-tender thyroid
  • Exophthalmos — proptosis (bulging eyes) from retro-orbital inflammation. May NOT resolve with treatment. Protect eyes: artificial tears, sunglasses, elevate HOB, tape eyelids at night if needed.
  • Pretibial myxedema — thickened, waxy skin on shins (dermopathy)

Labs Specific to Grave's:

  • TSI (Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulin) — POSITIVE (diagnostic)
  • Anti-TPO antibodies — often positive
  • Radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) — INCREASED diffuse uptake

🧊 Hashimoto's Thyroiditis — Deep Dive

HYPOTHYROID AUTOIMMUNE

Pathophysiology: Autoimmune destruction of thyroid tissue by anti-TPO (anti-thyroid peroxidase) antibodies and lymphocytic infiltration. Gradual, progressive thyroid failure. Most common cause of hypothyroidism in the US.

Unique Features:

  • Painless goiter (firm, rubbery) — may be initial presentation before hypothyroid symptoms develop
  • May have transient hyperthyroidism early (Hashitoxicosis) as stored hormone is released from damaged cells
  • Family history of autoimmune disease common

Labs Specific to Hashimoto's:

  • Anti-TPO antibodies — POSITIVE (highly diagnostic)
  • Anti-thyroglobulin antibodies — often positive
  • TSH ↑, T4 ↓ — classic primary hypothyroid pattern
🌪️ Thyroid Storm VS 🥶 Myxedema Coma
Both are LIFE-THREATENING emergencies requiring ICU-level care!
Feature 🌪️ Thyroid Storm 🥶 Myxedema Coma
Base Condition Extreme hyperthyroidism crisis Extreme hypothyroidism crisis
Trigger Infection, surgery, trauma, DKA, stopping antithyroid meds, iodine contrast, labor/delivery Infection, cold exposure, sedatives/opioids, surgery, stopping levothyroxine, MI
Temperature Hyperpyrexia (>104°F/40°C) — extreme fever Hypothermia (<95°F/35°C) — cannot generate heat
Heart Severe tachycardia (>140), AFib, HF, hypertension → cardiovascular collapse Severe bradycardia, hypotension, pericardial effusion → cardiovascular collapse
Neuro Agitation, delirium, psychosis, seizures → coma Lethargy, confusion, stupor → coma, hypoventilation
GI N/V, diarrhea, abdominal pain, jaundice (liver failure) Paralytic ileus, abdominal distension, constipation
Mortality 20-30% even with treatment 30-60% even with treatment
💊 Treatment
  1. PTU (blocks T4→T3 conversion) — loading dose
  2. Potassium iodide (SSKI) — given 1 hour AFTER PTU
  3. Propranolol (IV) — control HR
  4. Hydrocortisone — prevent adrenal crisis
  5. Cooling blankets (NOT aspirin — displaces T4 from proteins)
  6. O2, IV fluids, glucose
  1. IV Levothyroxine — cautious, slow replacement
  2. IV Hydrocortisone — prevent adrenal crisis
  3. Passive rewarming (blankets, warm room — NOT active rewarming)
  4. Ventilatory support (CO2 retention)
  5. IV fluids (cautious — risk of water intoxication)
  6. Monitor for seizures
NCLEX Key — Thyroid Storm: Do NOT give aspirin — it displaces T4 from binding proteins, making storm WORSE. Use acetaminophen for fever. Give PTU BEFORE iodine (otherwise iodine feeds the overactive thyroid).
NCLEX Key — Myxedema Coma: Do NOT actively rewarm (heating blankets, warm bath) — causes peripheral vasodilation → cardiovascular collapse. Use passive rewarming only. IV levothyroxine must be given SLOWLY — too fast causes fatal arrhythmias.
2 🎭 Adrenal Disorders
🌕 Cushing's Syndrome VS 🌑 Addison's Disease
Memory Aid — Think CORTISOL:
Cushing's = TOO MUCH cortisol → Cushing = Cortisol eXcess (think: "Cushy" = full/round/excess)
Addison's = NOT ENOUGH cortisol → Addison = Adrenal Deficiency (think: "Add-it-on" = needs to add cortisol)
Feature 🌕 Cushing's Syndrome 🌑 Addison's Disease
Pathophysiology Excess cortisol — from pituitary adenoma (Cushing's Disease), adrenal tumor, ectopic ACTH, or prolonged exogenous steroid use (most common cause!) Adrenocortical insufficiency — autoimmune destruction of adrenal cortex (most common), TB, hemorrhage. Loss of cortisol, aldosterone, AND androgens.
Appearance Moon face, buffalo hump (dorsocervical fat pad), truncal obesity with thin extremities, purple striae on abdomen/thighs Weight loss, muscle wasting, bronze/hyperpigmentation (↑ACTH stimulates melanocytes — especially skin creases, buccal mucosa, nipples, scars)
Vitals BP (hypertension) — cortisol enhances catecholamine effects BP (hypotension, orthostatic) — loss of aldosterone + cortisol
Fluid Status Fluid retention, edema, weight gain (cortisol has mineralocorticoid activity) Dehydration, hypovolemia, weight loss (loss of aldosterone → can't retain Na+/H2O)
Glucose ↑ Hyperglycemia (cortisol promotes gluconeogenesis) — "steroid diabetes" ↓ Hypoglycemia (loss of cortisol impairs gluconeogenesis)
Immune Immunosuppressed — ↑ infection risk, delayed wound healing, ↑ WBC (false leukocytosis) Normal to slightly ↑ immune function
Skin Thin, fragile skin; easy bruising; hirsutism (excess hair); acne; purple striae Bronze hyperpigmentation; vitiligo (autoimmune); ↓ body hair (↓ androgens)
Neuro/Psych Euphoria → depression, psychosis, insomnia, emotional lability, cognitive impairment Fatigue, weakness, depression, irritability, confusion
Bones Osteoporosis — cortisol inhibits osteoblasts, promotes calcium loss Generally preserved (no excess cortisol)
GI Peptic ulcer risk (↓ mucosal protection), GI bleeding Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, abdominal pain, diarrhea
🧪 Cortisol ↑ ELEVATED (24-hour urine cortisol elevated) ↓ LOW (AM cortisol depressed)
🧪 ACTH Depends on cause:
↑ if pituitary/ectopic (ACTH-dependent)
↓ if adrenal tumor or exogenous steroids		 ↑ ELEVATED (pituitary compensating for failed adrenals)
🧪 Na+ ↑ Hypernatremia (cortisol retains Na+) ↓ Hyponatremia (can't retain Na+ without aldosterone)
🧪 K+ ↓ Hypokalemia (cortisol wastes K+) ↑ Hyperkalemia (can't excrete K+ without aldosterone)
🧪 Glucose ↑ Hyperglycemia ↓ Hypoglycemia
🧪 Ca++ ↑ Hypercalcemia (bone resorption) Usually normal
Diagnostic Test Dexamethasone suppression test — cortisol does NOT suppress (positive)
24-hr urine free cortisol ↑
Midnight salivary cortisol ↑		 ACTH stimulation (Cosyntropin) test — cortisol does NOT rise (positive)
AM cortisol <3 is diagnostic
💊 Treatment
  • Address cause: remove tumor, taper exogenous steroids
  • Ketoconazole — suppresses cortisol synthesis
  • Surgery: transsphenoidal hypophysectomy (pituitary), adrenalectomy
  • Radiation if surgery not possible
  • Will need lifetime cortisol replacement after adrenalectomy
  • Hydrocortisone (Cortef) — cortisol replacement (⅔ AM, ⅓ PM)
  • Fludrocortisone (Florinef) — aldosterone replacement
  • Lifelong therapy — NEVER stop abruptly
  • Increase dose 2-3× during stress/illness/surgery
  • Medical alert bracelet essential
Cushing's Electrolyte Memory: Cortisol acts like aldosterone — retains Na+ & H2O, wastes K+.
Think: "Cushing Keeps Sodium, Kicks out Potassium" → ↑Na+, ↓K+, ↑glucose, ↑BP

Addison's Electrolyte Memory: Opposite of Cushing's — lost aldosterone means:
Think: "Addison Adds Potassium, Subtracts Sodium" → ↓Na+, ↑K+, ↓glucose, ↓BP

⚡ Addisonian Crisis (Acute Adrenal Crisis)

🚨 EMERGENCY CORTISOL DEFICIT

Trigger: Stress (infection, trauma, surgery) in undiagnosed Addison's OR abrupt withdrawal of exogenous steroids in steroid-dependent patient.

Signs — Think "Circulatory Collapse":

  • Profound hypotension / shock — may not respond to vasopressors without cortisol
  • Severe hypoglycemia
  • Hyperkalemia → cardiac arrhythmias
  • Severe dehydration, N/V, abdominal pain, fever, confusion → coma

Emergency Treatment:

  1. IV Hydrocortisone 100mg bolus STAT, then 50-100mg q6-8h
  2. Aggressive IV NS (0.9% NaCl) with dextrose (D5NS)
  3. Treat hyperkalemia (kayexalate, insulin/glucose)
  4. Identify and treat trigger (antibiotics if infection)
NEVER abruptly discontinue corticosteroids! Chronic steroid use suppresses the HPA axis. Abrupt withdrawal can precipitate Addisonian crisis and death. Always TAPER steroids gradually.
3 💧 ADH Disorders — SIADH vs. Diabetes Insipidus
💧 SIADH (Excess ADH) VS 🏜️ Diabetes Insipidus
Memory Aid:
SIADH = Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH = TOO MUCH ADH → retains too much water → dilutional hyponatremia
DI = Diabetes Insipidus = NOT ENOUGH ADH (or kidneys ignore it) → loses too much water → dehydration & hypernatremia
Feature 💧 SIADH (Excess ADH) 🏜️ Diabetes Insipidus (Deficient ADH)
Pathophysiology Excessive ADH secretion → kidneys retain water → dilutional hyponatremia. Causes: lung cancer (SCLC #1), brain injury, pneumonia, meds (SSRIs, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide). Central DI: pituitary doesn't produce ADH (head trauma, surgery, tumor).
Nephrogenic DI: kidneys resist ADH (lithium #1, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia).
Fluid Balance Fluid overload — water retention, but NO edema (dilutional). Weight gain. Concentrated urine, dilute serum. Severe dehydration — massive water loss. Weight loss. Dilute urine, concentrated serum.
Urine Output ↓ Decreased & CONCENTRATED
Urine specific gravity >1.030
Urine osmolality >100		 ↑ Massive polyuria (4-20 L/day!) & DILUTE
Urine specific gravity <1.005
Urine osmolality <200
Thirst Normal or ↓ (dilute serum suppresses thirst) Extreme polydipsia (raging thirst)
🧪 Serum Na+ ↓ HYPONATREMIA (<135) — the hallmark. Dilutional. ↑ HYPERNATREMIA (>145) — water loss concentrates sodium
🧪 Serum Osm ↓ LOW (<280) — dilute serum ↑ HIGH (>300) — concentrated serum
🧪 Urine Osm ↑ HIGH — kidneys concentrate urine ↓ LOW — kidneys can't concentrate
🧪 Urine Sp Gr ↑ HIGH (>1.030) ↓ LOW (<1.005)
Neuro Symptoms Confusion, HA, lethargy, N/V, seizures (if Na+ <120) → coma. All from brain cell swelling (cerebral edema). Weakness, fatigue, confusion (from dehydration). Less neuro risk unless Na+ rises rapidly.
💊 Treatment
  1. Fluid restriction (500-1000 mL/day) — #1 intervention
  2. Demeclocycline — blocks ADH action on kidneys
  3. Conivaptan / Tolvaptan — ADH receptor antagonists
  4. Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) — ONLY if symptomatic seizures. Raise Na+ no faster than 8-12 mEq/L per 24 hours
  5. Treat underlying cause
 Central DI: Desmopressin (DDAVP) — synthetic ADH replacement (intranasal, IV, or PO)

Nephrogenic DI:
  • Thiazide diuretics (HCTZ) — paradoxically ↓ urine output
  • Low-sodium diet
  • Remove offending drug (lithium)

Both types: Adequate fluid replacement, strict I&O, daily weights
NCLEX Key — SIADH: Think "Swimming In ADH" — too much water retained. Key danger is hyponatremia → seizures. Correct Na+ SLOWLY — rapid correction causes osmotic demyelination syndrome (central pontine myelinolysis) which is devastating and irreversible.
NCLEX Key — DI: Think "Dry & Insatiably thirsty." Monitor for dehydration, weight loss, tachycardia, poor skin turgor. Water deprivation test is diagnostic: if urine stays dilute after fluid restriction → DI confirmed. If DDAVP corrects it → Central DI. If not → Nephrogenic DI.
4 🦴 Parathyroid Disorders
☀️ Hyperparathyroidism VS ❄️ Hypoparathyroidism
Memory Aid — PTH raises calcium:
Hyperparathyroidism = TOO MUCH PTH → ↑ Ca++ (bones release calcium → "bones, stones, groans, moans")
Hypoparathyroidism = NOT ENOUGH PTH → ↓ Ca++ (tetany, spasms, seizures → "CATS go numb")
Feature ☀️ Hyperparathyroidism ❄️ Hypoparathyroidism
Pathophysiology Excess PTH → pulls Ca++ from bones, ↑ renal Ca++ reabsorption, ↑ intestinal Ca++ absorption. Usually parathyroid adenoma. Insufficient PTH → Ca++ stays in bones, ↑ renal Ca++ excretion, ↓ absorption. Usually post-thyroidectomy (#1 cause).
🧪 Calcium ↑ Hypercalcemia ↓ Hypocalcemia
🧪 Phosphorus ↓ Hypophosphatemia (inverse relationship) ↑ Hyperphosphatemia (inverse relationship)
🧪 PTH ↑ Elevated ↓ Low or absent
Mnemonic "Bones, Stones, Groans, Moans"
Bones: fractures, bone pain, osteoporosis
Stones: kidney stones (Ca++ deposits)
Groans: abdominal pain, constipation, N/V, pancreatitis
Moans: fatigue, depression, confusion, psychosis		 "CATS go numb"
Convulsions (seizures)
Arrhythmias (prolonged QT)
Tetany (Trousseau's & Chvostek's signs)
Spasms & Stridor (laryngospasm)
Cardiac Shortened QT, bradycardia, heart block, cardiac arrest at extreme levels Prolonged QT, ventricular arrhythmias, cardiac arrest
Neuromuscular Muscle weakness, fatigue, decreased DTRs, hypotonia Tetany, muscle spasms, hyperreflexia, Trousseau's sign (carpal spasm with BP cuff), Chvostek's sign (facial twitch on tap)
Renal Kidney stones, polyuria, polydipsia, nephrocalcinosis Decreased urine calcium
💊 Treatment Acute hypercalcemia:
  1. IV NS (aggressive hydration)
  2. Furosemide (Lasix) — promotes Ca++ excretion
  3. Calcitonin — rapid Ca++ lowering
  4. Bisphosphonates — inhibit bone resorption
Definitive: Parathyroidectomy 		Acute hypocalcemia:
• IV Calcium gluconate (slow IV push with cardiac monitoring!)
• Seizure precautions, airway management
Chronic:
• Oral calcium + Vitamin D (Calcitriol)
• Lifelong supplementation
NCLEX Key — Post-Thyroidectomy: Monitor for accidental parathyroid removal. Check Ca++ levels q4-6h. Keep IV calcium gluconate at bedside! Watch for tingling, numbness, Trousseau's, Chvostek's signs — these indicate hypocalcemia. Also watch for laryngeal nerve damage (hoarseness) and hemorrhage (check behind neck, sensation of tightness).
5 ⚡ Pheochromocytoma

⚡ Pheochromocytoma

CATECHOLAMINE EXCESS HYPERTENSIVE CRISIS RISK

Pathophysiology: Rare catecholamine-secreting tumor of the adrenal medulla (90%) or extra-adrenal paraganglia. Produces massive amounts of epinephrine and norepinephrine → severe, paroxysmal hypertension.

The "5 Ps" of Pheochromocytoma:

  • Pressure (severe hypertension — intermittent or sustained, BP >200 systolic)
  • Pain (severe headache — pounding)
  • Perspiration (profuse diaphoresis)
  • Palpitations (tachycardia, arrhythmias)
  • Pallor (vasoconstriction → pale face)

Labs:

  • 24-hour urine metanephrines & VMA — ↑ ELEVATED (gold standard)
  • Plasma free metanephrines — ↑ ELEVATED (screening test)
  • ↑ Glucose (catecholamines promote glycogenolysis)

Treatment:

  1. Phenoxybenzamine (Dibenzyline) — alpha-blocker (given FIRST, 10-14 days pre-op)
  2. Propranolol — beta-blocker (given AFTER alpha-blockade established)
  3. Surgical removal (adrenalectomy) — definitive cure
CRITICAL: NEVER give beta-blockers BEFORE alpha-blockers in pheochromocytoma! Beta-blockade alone → unopposed alpha stimulation → severe hypertensive crisis → stroke/death. Always alpha-block FIRST, then add beta-blocker.
NCLEX Key: Avoid triggers: abdominal palpation, Valsalva maneuver, heavy lifting, emotional stress, tyramine-rich foods. Never palpate the abdomen of a suspected pheochromocytoma patient — can trigger lethal hypertensive crisis.

7 🧠 High-Yield NCLEX Memory Anchors

🔥 "Hot T" → Hyperthyroidism

  • Hot, sweaty, tachycardic
  • Tremors, thin (weight loss)
  • TSH is Tiny (low)
  • T3/T4 are Towering (high)

🧊 "Cold T" → Hypothyroidism

  • Cold, constipated, chunky (weight gain)
  • Tired, thick skin, thinning hair
  • TSH is Tremendous (high)
  • T3/T4 are Tiny (low)

🌕 "Cushy Cushing's"

  • Cushy = round (moon face, buffalo hump)
  • Sugar UP (hyperglycemia)
  • Sodium UP, Potassium DOWN
  • BP UP, immune DOWN

🌑 "Add-it-on Addison's"

  • Needs to "add" cortisol back
  • Sugar DOWN (hypoglycemia)
  • Sodium DOWN, Potassium UP
  • BP DOWN, skin DARK (bronze)

💧 "Swimming in ADH" → SIADH

  • Retains water → dilutes sodium
  • Low Na+, low serum osmolality
  • Concentrated urine, ↓ output
  • Treatment: RESTRICT fluids

🏜️ "Dry & Insipid" → DI

  • Loses water → concentrates sodium
  • High Na+, high serum osmolality
  • Dilute urine, MASSIVE output
  • Treatment: REPLACE fluids + DDAVP

🦴 "Bones Stones Groans Moans"

  • HYPERparathyroid = HYPERcalcemia
  • Bones break, stones form, belly groans, patient moans
  • PTH ↑, Ca++ ↑, PO4 ↓

😾 "CATS go numb"

  • HYPOparathyroid = HYPOcalcemia
  • Convulsions, Arrhythmias, Tetany, Spasms
  • Trousseau's & Chvostek's signs positive
  • PTH ↓, Ca++ ↓, PO4 ↑
Final NCLEX Tip: When comparing any two endocrine disorders, start with the hormone — is it excess or deficit? Then think through the downstream effects on metabolism, electrolytes, vitals, and target organs. Endocrine disorders almost always come in opposite pairs: hyper/hypo, excess/deficit. Master one, and you automatically know the other by reversing everything.
